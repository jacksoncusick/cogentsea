Truth be told, the only real problem I have with creating an Avatar is that the concept normally trips people up. If it helps you, go for it. But if a concept doesn't work, don't be afraid to throw it out the window.
You hear it all the time -- the market chooses, not you. The only real way to know, is to test. Avatars tend to hold a lot of copywriters back from writing and putting things out in the world.
And there's an easier way - as you'll see in my research process below.
.
.
Yesterday, I told you there are research shortcuts.
These shortcuts are tied to this RESEARCH MAXIM (a maxim is short, pithy statement expressing a general truth or rule of conduct.)
 
Research Maxim: 

The majority of your research has already been done by someone else

I don't care if you're launching a new product the world has never seen, another writer, marketer, or company has already compiled most of what you need to know (including what you need to know about your customer aka Avatar).
Someone else has done 90% of the job uncovering pain points and finding solutions (and a lot of times your job is to simply fill in that 10% gap).
The research process below will work for a wide-range of people:
• You have no idea what to create. 
• You have an idea but no product yet. 
• You have a product.
Just take out a pen & piece of paper and follow along.
PHASE ONE: FOCUS YOUR BRAIN 

Phase One will help you focus your brain on what's going to be important when you actually start drilling down and researching.
 
Based on your Market Sophistication & Awareness Level (explained below), you'll know what things you're trying to extract (ie, an ingredient in your product that you can make a secret.) You'll figure out how you can differentiate your product based on what's being promised and claimed in the world.
+++++
Step 0: Read books on selling and consumer pyschology
Apologies for starting you off with a non-step... but this the foundation of any sales message. Understanding sales and pyschology is going to help your entire research process.
If you don't have a sound understanding of sales principles, then it's going to be harder to know what to look for.
And the best part -- this understanding can eliminate the need for 1 specific Avatar -- if you know sales you're going to know how to write in a way that will attract just about any Avatar you could cook up.
For a list of essential books on copywriting & sales, go here: http://copyhour.com/reading-list
+++++
Step 1: What's the product?
A) What's the product you'll be selling?
Is it a book, course, physical product, strategy session, etc. Just write down the product type.
B) Does your product exist already?
Is it selling or capable of being sold right now?
Yes or no.
+++++
Step 2: What's your Market Sophistication?
Answer this: 
1. Stage 1: Are you first to market? Is your product/service brand new? Your market will have never seen this product before.
2. Stage 2: Are you second-ish to market? You have a newer product but there are a few competitors?
3. Stage 3: Are you in a market that's crowded and people are becoming jaded with big claims? 
If you're in Stage 1 - first to bring a product to market - all you need to do is tell people what your product can do. Name the Features & Benefits of your product
If you're in Stage 2 - newer product but there are competitors - you need to enlarge your Claims. Make bolder Claims than your competition. Is your product better, faster, stronger than competitors? Put some measurability to it to one up your competitors.
If you're in Stage 3 - the market is getting jaded with exaggerated claims - you'll need to choose a marketing angle which highlights your Features differently.
 
+++++
Step 3: What's your Medium?
How exactly will you be delivering your sales message?
A) Will you be selling through an online sales letter (or VSL)? Through a direct mail package? An email series? A phone consultation? Google Adwords banner to a sales page?
B) If you're unsure - what are your direct competitors doing? What are your indirect competitors doing (companies in your market that aren't selling the same exact product)?
Don't reinvent the wheel at this stage. If no one is selling via SnapChat -- it might be an opportunity -- but it might also be a fool's errand. You can get creative after you've got a proven converting funnel in place. 
+++++
Step 4: Loosely pick an Awareness Level
Based on your Market sophistication above and your Medium -- what Awareness Level do you think you'll target first?

If you're unsure, error on the right side of the scale.
+++++
All Phase One has done is focus your brain on what to look for during your research.
+++++
PHASE TWO: MARKET & COMPETITIVE RESEARCH

Remember our Research Maxim: The majority of your research has already been done by someone else
(Step 5 below is the biggest step in this process. It might seem daunting at first, but when you jump in - it's actually very simple.)
+++++
 
Step 5: Discovery & Offer Extraction
A) Find your direct competitor's sales materials (competitors selling the same exact type of product as you. ie. An ebook on getting your ex back.)
Use Adbeat.com if you can't find them or don't know who the top companies are.
--> Pay closest attention to the sales materials that match your chosen medium (sales letter or VSL).
--> Hand copy the sales messages of the top 1-3 competitors.
--> Write their headlines and subheadlines down on notecards (1 headline per notecard)
--> Look for their Promises. What are they Promising their product or company can do. Write one Promise on a notecard (the best sales letters will have 1 main promise stated in several different ways. If you can't find a ton of unique Promises, it's okay).
--> Look for Claims (a claim is a statement that supports a Promise). Write the Claims that support a Promise on the back of the Promise notecard.
--> Now look for Proof that supports the Claims (studies, testimonials, articles, etc.) If there's room, write these Proof elements on the back of the same Promise notecard.
--> Look at their Offer. An Offer is just the Features that make up a product. 
--> Write each Feature of their product on a notecard.
--> On the backside of your Feature notecard, write the Benefits they say that product provides (there will be overlap with the Promises, Claims, & Proof from above)
--> Look for any unique-to-their-product Objections they handle. What's an Objection? Read this:https://blog.crazyegg.com/2013/04/24/overcome-objections/
--> Look for customer demographics if possible ("Are you a senior over 65?"). Write these down on a notecard.
B) (Especially if you don't have direct competitors) Look at indirect competitors -- the top 1-3 most successful companies in the same market that don't have the same product type.
Repeat the steps above.
Note: If you are brand new, first to market... and you don't have any other competitors to look at... look at other companies who are using the same Medium as you who you'd like to model.
Use the steps above - especially the handwriting of their sales message in order to get a deeper understanding of how they're selling.
+++++
STEP 6: Deep(er) Dive
Doing the above should give you about 60-80% of the research you'll need.
In this stage you're going deeper. Remember what your brain should be focused on from PHASE ONE.
A) Find studies related to your product. Look for scientific journals. Write interesting facts & discoveries down on notecards (remember what you're looking for)
B) Gather your testimonials (if you have them)
C) Gather 3rd party credibility you could use (ie "The New York Times said, 'X,y,z'.")
C) Conduct surveys (if possible). Survey your audience a la Ryan Levesque's Ask Method.
D) Read blogs & articles that cover your topic both directly and indirectly (Again looking for Headlines, Promises, Claims, and Proof).
E) Read forum posts and Reddit discussions taking notes.
F) Look for STORIES during this entire process. What stories could you tell?
 
Put everything on notecards.
+++++
At this stage in the game, you've knocked out your competitive research and gathered a lot of information. Start flipping through your notecards (and I got this from Gary Halbert) saying, "Hmmm... interesting."
+++++
PHASE THREE: YOUR PRODUCT & OFFER

+++++
 
Step 7: Build your offer
A) Consume your product. Read it. Use it. Get it delivered and open it. 
Read through the course/book at least once. 
B) Read it again
--> Take note of the Features of the product. Put each Feature on its own notecard.
--> On the backside, write out potential Benefits that Feature can provide to the customer. Those Benefits are your Claims. What's a piece of supporting Proof you can give to that claim. Write it down.
You want everything on a note card so you can just flip through them.
--> Take note of any stories or unique ancedotes you find. What Objections do you see addressed within your product? (ie, "But what if I'm busy?" Well, chapter 2 talks about solving a certain issue in 5 minutes).
Now you have your Offer -- a list of your Features, their Benefits (Claims + Proof) -- and you have your Objections. 
C) If you don't have a product at this point, build what would be an "Ideal Offer"
What Features would you include in an ideal world? Put them on notecards.
Don't judge just yet, you likely won't be able to include all the Features you've made up in this Ideal Offer. You can remove Features you're incapable of creating later.
+++++
 
PHASE FOUR: THINK THEN REST 

+++++
Step 8: Say "Hmm..."
Constantly flip through your competitor's notecards and your Offer notecards. 
"Mind the gaps" - what Features do they have that you don't? What Promises are they making that you can't?
The gap might be where your opportunity is. The gap might signal the differentiation that you can point to.
The gap might signal that you need to add more Features to your Offer (or maybe you could build a bonus report).
+++++
Step 9: Rest
At this point you've done a lot and you have a lot to consider. It's time to let your unconsious brain take over. 
This will be an individual experience but get out of the office. Go exercise.
Take the week or the weekend off of writing and researching.
Inspiration will hit at some point. When it does, listen to it.
Go write using the Sales Message Oultine structure I've provided in CopyHour.
+++++
And that's it!
